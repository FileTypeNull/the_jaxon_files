Work, Energy, and Power

Vocabulary

  • Work: the energy used by a force to move an object a certain distance

  • Energy: the ability to perform work

  • Power: the amount of work done over a certain time interval


Work can be derived by finding the area under a force-displacement curve. Therefore, the formuala work is W = Fdcosθ. The cosθ comes from finding the force vector in the direction of motion rather than the direction of the force.
The energy we will be working with in mechanics is mechanical energy, which is the energy of movement. There are two types of mechanical energy: potential and kinetic. Kinetic energy is the amount of energy an object has as it moves, represented by K. Potential energy is the amount of energy an object has as it is stationary (essentially, the amount of stored energy it has that it can use to move), represented by U. The following are the formulae:
  K = ½mvf2
  U = mgh
Due to the laws of the universe, there must be a conservation of all the energy in the universe is constant. In mechanics, this is known as the law of conservation of mechanical energy, where mechanical energy is conserved unless there is an external force doing negative work on the system (usually, friction). This law is represented by the equation: E0 = Ef
Work can also be defined as the change in kinetic energy, or ΔK.

Take a look at the following roller coaster track:

If there were a coaster cart of mass M on the top of the track at height H, what would its speed be at the top of the loop if the radius of the loop is R? Assume friction is negligible and the cart starts at rest.
The first thing we should do to answer this question is to calculate the initial mechanical energy at the top of the track. Knowing that E (mechanical energy) is the sum of kinetic energy and potential energy (E = K + U) and that the cart starts at rest (so, K = 0), we know that the initial mechanical energy is equal to the potential energy (mgh). The initial potential energy, and initial mechanical energy, of the cart is MgH.
The final mechanical energy is equal to the sum of the kinetic energy at the top of the loop and the potential energy at the top of the loop. If we say that the bottom of the track is at height 0, then the top of the loop is at height 2R. This means the cart's potential energy at this point is 2mgR. The kinetic energy at the top of the loop is ½mv2. Since we are trying to find the speed at the top of the loop, let's solve for v.
Our current conservation equation can be modeled as such:
  mgH = 2mgR + ½mv2
If we isolate the final kinetic energy:
  ½mv2 = mgH − 2mgR
Now, we solve for v:
  mv2 = 2mgH − 4mgR → v2 = 2g(H − 2R) → v = √(2g(H − 2R))
We can say that H − 2R is our overall change in height, Δh. Therefore, we can rewrite this as: v = √(2gΔh).

The formula for power is: P = W⁄Δt
