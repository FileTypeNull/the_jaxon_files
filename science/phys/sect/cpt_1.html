Visualizing the First and Second Kinematics

The first and second kinematics are the base equations, meaning the are not derived from other formulae. But how do these kinematics work and what is their purpose? Well, both kinematics relate to some function on a velocity-time graph. The area underneath the function is the displacment and the slope of the line is the acceleration. In terms of calculus, the area is an integral and the slope is a derivative.

I.

We know that the slope of the function is acceleration (because a = Δv⁄t ) and therefore can come up with a kind of linear equation for the funtion below:

Let's start with our linear equation: y = b + mx. Our "m" is the acceleration, so we'll replace it with a. The "b" is the initial veloity, so we'll replace it with v0.
  → y = v0 + ax
The end point of the funtion is (t, vf) when put into a coordinate pair of (x, y). Therefore, we can replace them accordingly:
  → vf = v0 + at

II.

What is the displacement in relation to a velocity-time graph? It is calulated from finding the area beneath the function. Take a look at the graph below:

To get the area under the function, I sliced the function into two pieces and made a rectangle (which is easier to calculate area for rather than a trapezoid) Notice where the cut took place—halfway between the final and initial velocities, which is the height of our newly-made rectangle. The width is time, t. The area of a rectangle is width times height...
  → Δx = ½(vf + v0)×t

You could also say that the equation comes directly from the formula for deriving the area of a trapezoid (see Alg II/Geo B). However, it is much easier to visualize in the manner that I described.



Determining the Third and Fourth Kinematics

The third and fourth kinematics are actually derived from the velocities in the first two kinematics. However, they can also be found graphically. These two kinematics will be very useful when we move on to two-dimensional kinematics.

I.

Method 1a: Replace the value of vf in the second kinematic with the definition of vf in the first kinematic.
Δx = ½((v0 + at) + v0)×t
  → Δx = ½(2v0 + at)×t
  → Δx = (v0 + ½at)×t
  → Δx = v0t + ½at²

Method 1b: Knowing that the area under a velocity-time graph is the displacement, use this graph to solve for displacment.

Our graph has endpoints (0, v0) and (t, vf). We can break the area beneath the line into two shapes: a right triangle and a rectangle:

The area of the rectangle can be represented as: v0×t
The area of the triangle can be represented as: ½Δv×t

Δv is the same as acceleration times time (at), so let's join the two areas together, substitute, and simplify.
  → Δx = v0×t + ½(at)×t
  → Δx = v0t + ½at×t
  → Δx = v0t + ½at²

II.

Method 2a: Replace the value of v0 in the second kinematic with the definition of v0 in the first kinematic.
Δx = ½(vf + (vf − at))×t
  → Δx = ½(2vf − at)×t
  → Δx = (vf − ½at)×t
  → Δx = vf t − ½at²

Method 2b: Knowing that the area under a velocity-time graph is the displacement, use this graph to solve for displacment.

Our graph has endpoints (0, v0) and (t, vf). We can ignore the fact that the area underneath the graph is a trapezoid and imagine that the shape is actually a rectangle surrounding the graph:

The area of this rectangle can be represented as vf ×t
However, we want to fiind the displacement under the graph, so we must subtract the area of the above triangle to get the value of the trapezoidal area. The area of that triangle is ½Δv×t.

All that's left is finding the difference, substituting Δv with at, and simplifying:
  → Δx = vf ×t − ½(at)×t
  → Δx = vf t − ½at×t
  → Δx = vf t − ½at²



Discovering the Fifth Kinematic

The fifth kinematic, vf ² − v0² = 2aΔx, looks a little strange. That is because it is a derived equation by using the definition of time from the first and second kinematics

Using the first kinematic, we can define time as t = (vf − v0)⁄a
Using the second kinematic, we can define time as t = 2Δx⁄(vf + v0)

Now we have two definitions for time. Thus, we can set them equal to each other: (vf − v0)⁄a = 2Δx⁄(vf + v0)

Finally, we make it look a little nicer by simplifying:
  → (vf − v0)(vf + v0) = 2aΔx
  → vf ² − v0² = 2aΔx

The fifth kinematic is extremely useful because, unlike the others, it does not rely on time.
