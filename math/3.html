Formulae

Distance=
P1P2 [Point 1, Point 2] = √[(x2-x1)2 + (y2-y1)2]

Midpoint=
M([x1+x2] ÷ 2, [y1+y2] ÷ 2)

Slope=
m = (y2 - y1) ÷ (x2 - x1)

Triangle Shoelace=
AREA = 1⁄2 × |(x1y2)-(x2y1) + (x2y3)-(x3y2) + (x3y1)-(x1y3)|

Quadrilateral Shoelace=
AREA = 1⁄2 × |(x1y2)-(x2y1) + (x2y3)-(x3y2) + (x3y4)-(x4y3) +(x4y1)-(x1y4)|

FOIL=
(x + y)2 = x2 + 2xy + y2

Internally Divided Segment=
P([(mx2+nx1) ÷ (m+n)], [(my2+ny1) ÷ (m+n)])

Externally Divided Segment=
P([(mx2-nx1) ÷ (m-n)], [(my2-ny1) ÷ (m-n)])

Slope-Intercept Form=
y = mx + b

Slope-Point Form=
y - y1 = m(x - x1)

Inscribed Angle=
i[inscribed] = 1⁄2 × c[central]

Arc Length=
length of A͡B = θ⁄360° × c[circumference]

Area of a Sector=
area of the sector CAB = θ⁄360° × a[area]

Proving a Right Triangle=

Method 1: Show two sides of the triangle are perpendicular by demonstrating their slopes are opposite reciprocals.
Method 2: Calculate the distances of all three sides and then test Pythagorean's theorem to show the three lengths make Pythagorean's theorem true.

Proving a Parallelogram=

Method 1: Show both pairs of opposite sides are parallel by showing they have equal slopes.
Method 2: Show that one pair of adjacent sides isn't perpendicular.
Method 3: Show that the diagonals aren't perpendicular.

Proving a Non-Square Rectangle=

Method 1: Show both pairs of opposite sides are parallel by showing they have equal slopes.
Method 2: Show that one pair of adjacent sides is perpendicular.
Method 3: Show that the diagonals aren't perpendicular.

Proving a Rhombus=

Method 1: Show both pairs of opposite sides are parallel by showing they have equal slopes.
Method 2: Show that one pair of adjacent sides isn't perpendicular.
Method 3: Show that the diagonals are perpendicular.

Proving a Square=

Method 1: Show both pairs of opposite sides are parallel by showing they have equal slopes.
Method 2: Show that one pair of adjacent sides is perpendicular.
Method 3: Show that the diagonals are perpendicular.

Proving a Regular Trapezoid=

Method 1: Prove that only there is only one pair of parallel sides.
Method 2: Prove that it is isosceles if the nonparallel sides are congruent (using distance formula).

Proving an Irregular Trapezoid=

Method 1: Prove that there are no pairs of parallel sides.
Method 2: Graph the points and find every side's rise⁄run; none of the sides will have the same rise⁄run.

Unit 1 - Euclidean Circles=

Vocabulary=
Arc: A part of the circumference of a circle or other curve.
Center: The center of the circle, usually symbolized by a dot.
Radius: The distance between the center and any point on the circle.
Diameter: A specific type of chord. It is the distance between one point on the circle and another point on the circle that passes through the center.
Chord: The distance between one point on the circle and another point on the circle.
Secant Line: A line that passes through the circle and touches two points on the circle.
Tangent Line: A line that touches one point on the circle.
Point of Tangency: The point that touches both the tangent line and the circle.
Common Tangent: A line that is tangent to two or more circles. They can be external or internal.
Sector: A portion, or slice, of the entire area of the circle.


Arcs=
An arc is a section of the circle that is separated from the rest of the circumference by two radii separated by a central angle. Arcs are measured in degrees because it is near impossible to find the length of a curve. The central angle is the angle that separates the two radii. Due to an arc being an angle, the measure of the central angle is equivalent to its arc.

There are three different arcs. There is the minor arc (written as A͡B), which means the central angle is less than 180o. There is the major arc (written as A͡C͡B), which means the central angle is greater than 180o. Finally, there is a semicircle, which is when the central angle is 180o, making the two radii a diameter.
There is also an inscribed angle, which created an arc that can also be called an intercepted arc. The inscribed angle's measure is half of the central angle or intercepted angle.

It is important to remember that any quadrilateral inside of a circle (known as a cyclic quadrilateral) has its opposite angles supplementary to each other.


Theorems=
  1. The angle between a tangent and a chord θ = mT͡P⁄2
  2. The angle between two chords θ = [mA͡C + mD͡B] ÷ 2
  3. The angle between two tangents θ = [mQ͡S͡R - mQ͡R] ÷ 2
  4. The angle between two secants θ = [mR͡T - mQ͡S] ÷ 2
  5. The angle between a tangent and a secant θ = [mT͡R - mT͡Q] ÷ 2
  6. A radius perpendicular to a chord bisects the chord
  7. Congruent chords are equidistant from the center
  8. Congruent chords intercept congruent arcs
  9. Parallel chords intercept congruent arcs
  10. Chord segments PA × PB = PC × PD
  11. Segments of secants PA × PB = PC × PD)
  12. Segments of a tangent and a secant PA × PB = PT2
  13. If two tangents intersect outside a circle, then the distances from the intersecting point and the points of tangency are congruent TP ≅ TQ

Unit 2 - Geometric Circles=

Standard Equation: (x - a)2 + (y - b)2 = r2, where (a,b) = center of circle; r = radius; (x,y) = any point on circle [left as variables]

If circle touches/is tangent to x = n(number), use the equation: r = |(a-n)|
If circle touches/is tangent to y = n(number), use the equation: r = |(b-n)|
The line x = 0 is the y-axis; the line y = 0 is the x-axis

If given area and center, use the equation πr2 = ga(given area)
If given circumference and center, use the equation: πd = gc(given circumference)

If given the two endpoints of the diameter, use the distance formula to find the diameter, then divide by 2 to find the radius. Next, find the midpoint of the diameter to find the center. Finally, input the radius and the center into the standard equation and you have your answer.

Unit 3 - Probability=

Sample Space: A list of all possible outcomes; normally listed in set notation S={A,B}
Outcome: The end result of a certain event
Set: A set is a well-defined collection of objects
Element: Each object in a set
Independent Events: An event whose probability does not change due to another event taking place P(A∩B) = P(A) ∩ P(B)
Dependent Events: An event whose probability does change due to another event taking place P(A∩B) = P(A) ∪ P(B|A)
Venn Diagram: A comparison of two or more events and their outcomes
Union: Comparison of the outcomes of EITHER set
Intersection: Comparison of the outcomes SHARED by each set
Complement: All outcomes that are NOT the event
Conditional Probability: The probability of an event given the probability of another event P(A|B) = P(A∩B) ÷ P(B)
Contingency Table: A table showing the distribution of one variable in rows and another in columns, used to study the association between the two variables.
Mutually Exclusive Events: Events that do not share any outcomes
Event: Desired outcome of an experiment

Imaginary Numbers=
Some imaginary numbers include: √-9, √-16, √-48, ∜-16, -2431/6, and -101/8.

Definition: (negative number)1/n, where "n" is both greater than or equal to 2 and is even.

i is the special imaginary number:
i = √-1
i2 = -1
i3 = -i
i4 = 1
i5 = i
i6 = -1

RULE: i positive multiple of 4 = 1, which can be implemented into the rules of exponents to find ALL values of i.

Other imaginary numbers are in terms of i, such as √-9 being equivalent to 3i because √-9 is equivalent to √-1 (or i) × √9

RULE: i will never be in the denominator, so 1⁄-i is equivalent to i

Complex Numbers=
DIVISION RULE: Multiply the denominator by its conjugate (a + ib → a - ib) [change the sign of the imaginary part] and the numerator by the denominator's conjugate

Example(s):
   Step 1: (6i + 7) ÷ (3 + 5i) [Restate the Problem]
   Step 2: (6i + 7)(3 - 5i) ÷ (3 + 5i)(3 - 5i) [Multiply the numerator and denominator by the denominator's conjugate]
   Step 3: (18i - 30i^2 - 35i) ÷ (9 - 15i + 15i - 25i^2) [FOIL]
   Step 4: (-17i + 51) ÷ (34) [Simplify]
   Step 5: (-i + 3) ÷ 2 [Simplify]

ABSOLUTE VALUE: Use the equation: √(x2 + y2), where x is the real number of the real part and y is the real number of the imaginary part.

Solving Quadratic Equations=

  1. Taking the Square Root:
    1. 1: Rewrite the Problem
    2. 2: Simplify the equation until you are left with var^2 = n(number)
    3. 3: Find the square root of either side
    4. 4: Rewrite the answer

      Example(s):
         Step 1: 4x2 + 1 = -63
         Step 2: x2 = -16
         Step 3: √x2 = √-16
         Step 4: x = ±4i

  2. Factorizing (a = 1):
    1. 1: Rewrite the Problem
    2. 2: Simplify so that n(number) is equal to 0 and a = 1
    3. 3: Find the values of b and c
    4. 4: Find two numbers whose sum is equal to the value of b and whose product is equal to c
    5. 5: Fill out the following equation: (var + num1)(var + num2) = 0
    6. 6: Both (var + num1) and (var + num2) are equal to 0, so use that fact to find both values of var
    7. 7: Rewrite the Answer

      Example(s):
         Step 1: b2 + 11b + 28 = 0
         Step 5: (b + 7)(b + 4) = 0
         Step 6: b + 7 = 0, b + 4 = 0
         Step 7: b = -7, -4

  3. Factorizing by Grouping (a ≠ 1)
    1. 1: Rewrite the Problem
    2. 2: Simplify so that n(number) is equal to 0
    3. 3: Multiply the values of a and c together [we'll call this P]
    4. 4: Using P and the value of b, find two numbers whose sum is equal to the value of b and whose product is equal to P
    5. 5: Fill out the following equation: (var + num1)(var + num2) = 0
    6. 6: Both (var + num1) and (var + num2) are equal to 0, so use that fact to find both values of var
    7. 7: Rewrite the Answer

      Example(s):
         Step 1: 2n2 + 11n + 14 = 0
         Step 5: (n + 7)(n + 4) = 0
         Step 6: n + 7 = 0, n + 4 = 0
         Step 7: n = -7, -4

Descartes' Rule of Sign=
Let P(x) of polynomial
a) The number of change of sign in P(x) is the maximum number of positive real roots or less by even numbers.
b) The number of change of sign in P(-x) is the maximum number of negative real roots or less by even numbers.

- Four Terms

Piecewise Functions=
Piecewise functions are exactly what their name suggests. A piecewise function does not, unlike the square root, rational, cube root, and absolute value functions, have a parent or transformational function. There are two different types of piecewise functions:
  • Continuous (e.g. absolute value function)
  • Discontinuous

Example(s):
  1. f(x) = {3x - 4 if 0 < x ≤ 3, 2x if -6 ≤ x ≤ 0}
  2. f(x) = {0 if 0 < x < 5, 10 if 5 ≤ x ≤ 11, 25 if 11 < x ≤ 17, 35 if x > 17}

Unit 8 - Exponential and Logarithmic Functions=

Exponential Functions=
An exponential function is any function that follows the transformational formula f(x) = a(b)x-h + k:
  • b: the base number, such as 2 in f(x) = 2x
  • x: the exponent and will be the x-variable in the function
  • a: the dilation/x-axis reflection factor:
  • h: the horizontal shift
  • k: the vertical shift
  • There is no y-axis reflection factor in an exponential function because a function can be reflected over the x-axis if the numerator and denominator of the base are switched

If the base of the exponential function is less than 1 but greater than 0, the function is known as exponential decay. If the base of the exponential function is greater than 1, the function is known as exponential growth.

Exponential functions also have a special number: e (known as Euler's number). Euler's number is about 2.718, but is best represented by 2 < e < 3. The function f(x) = ex is a special exponential growth function using Euler's number. The function f(x) = e-x is a special exponential decay function using Euler's number. The transformational function using Euler's number is f(x) = a(e)±x-h + k.

You can find the characteristics of an exponential function using these formulae:
  • Domain: (-∞, +∞)
  • Range: (k, ±∞)
  • Horizontal Asymptote: y = k
  • X-Intercept: (logb(-k⁄a) + h, 0)
  • Y-Intercept: Find f(0)

Logarithmic Functions=
Logarithmic functions are the inverses of exponential functions. If the exponential parent function is f(x) = bx, then the logarithmic parent function is x = logb[f(x)]. In the logarithmic parent function, x represents the exponent, b is the base, and f(x) is the x-coordinate on the graph. The value inside of the logarithm (x - h) must NEVER be ≤ 0. The rules regarding the base of an exponential function still apply to the base of a logarithmic function. In an actual logarithmic function, x is referred to as f(x) (OR) y, and f(x) is referred to as x.

There are two types of special logarithmic functions: common logarithm and natural logarithm. A common logarithm is a logarithmic function with the base 10 (i.e. f(x) = log10(x)). In the common logarithm, there is no need to write the base, so it is written as log(x). A natural logarithm is a logarithmic function with the base e (i.e. f(x) = loge(x)). In the natural logarithm, the "log" is written as "ln", which stands for natural logarithm (just backwards).

You can find the characteristics of a logarithmic function using these formulae:
  • Domain: (h, ±∞)
  • Range: (-∞, +∞)
  • Vertical Asymptote: y = h
  • X-Intercept: (b-k + k, 0)
  • Y-Intercept: Find f(0)
