The Demand for Resources

Firms need resources in order to produce products. They meet the households at the factor market (which is not a real place, like the black market) and pay them for these resources. The demand for resources is a derived demand, derived from the products that they help produce. The demand for a product influences the demand of the resources used to produce said product.

Marginal Revenue Product

The strength of the demand for any resource will depend on the productivity of the resource and the market value of the good or service it helps to produce. This is just a more definitional version of the statements I made before. Marginal Revenue Product (MRP) is defined as the change in total revenue resulting from the use of each additional unit of a resource. In a mathematical sense, this means that MRP = ΔTR ÷ ΔQr, where TR is the total revenue and Qr is the resource quantity.

The rule for employing resources is when MRP = MRC, where MRC is the marginal resource cost. The marginal resource cost is the amount that each additional unit of a resource adds to the firm's total (resource) cost. Mathematically, MRC = ΔTCr ÷ ΔQr.

Know that resource demand is the same as the MRP in a competitive product market.

Optimal Combination of Resources

Firms must answer two questions:
  1. What combination of resources will minimize costs at a specific level of output?
  2. What combination of resources will maximize profit?
The least-cost combinaion of resources is employed when the last dollar spent on each resource yields the same marginal product. Confusing, huh? Here's a formula:

Marginal Productivity Theory of Income Distribution

In the marginal productivity theory of income distribution, income gets distributed accordingly to contribution of society's output. Therefore, income payments based on marginal revenue product seem to provide a fair and equitable distribution of society's income. There are several critiques of this theory despite its seemingly fair principles. Such criticism include: inequality and market imperfections.

Wage Rates

Wage is the resource price of labor. As the quantity of labor increases, the real wage rate increases as well. We can say that the wage rate represents the MRC. A wage rate is the price paid per unit of labor services, in this case an hour of work. A nominal wage is the amount of money received per hour, day, or year. A real wage is the quantity of goods and services a worker can obtain with nominal wages; real wages reveal the "purchasing power" of nominal wages (McConnell & Brue 521).

When workers produce more quantity of product (meaning an increase in productivity), there will be more real income available for distribution. There is a close relationship between output and compensation. We can never infer that an increase in wage rates will lead to an increase in productivity, however. A high correlation is not the same as true causation.

Monopsony Model

The difference between a perfectly competitive market and a monopsony is that the monopsony is a market in which a single employer of labor has substantial hiring power. In this model, the MRC is geater than the wage rate. This is because there must be uniform distribution of income. This is similar to the model of nondiscriminatory monopoly.
