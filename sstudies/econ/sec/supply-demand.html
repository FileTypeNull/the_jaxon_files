Demand

Demand is the desire for a product dependent upon its quantity and price. The Law of Demand states that (keeping all other things equal) as the price falls, the quantity demanded rises. As the price rises, the quantity demanded falls. This is an inverse relationship which can be seen in the graph below that is usually used to represent the market demand. Market demand is the representation of the demands of multiple individuals (individual demand is also called quantity demanded).

Changes in demand:
  • A change in quantity demanded results in a different price along the same curve
  • A change in market demand results in the same price on a different curve



Demand is influenced by various determinants:
  • Buyers' tastes
  • Number of buyers
  • Income
  • Prices of related goods
  • Consumer expectations

When the demand increases, the curve shifts to the right. When the demand decreases, the curve shifts to the left.

Supply

Supply is the amount of a product available in the market at a certain production quantity and selling price. The Law of Supply states that (keeping all other things equal) as the price falls, the quantity supplied falls. As the price rises, the quantity supplied rises. This is a direct relationship which can be seen in the graph below that is usually used to represent the market supply.

Changes in supply:
  • A change in quantity supplied results in a different price along the same curve
  • A change in market supply results in the same price on a different curve



Supply is influenced by various determinants:
  • Resource prices
  • Technology
  • Taxes and subsidies
  • Prices of other goods
  • Producer expectations
  • Number of suppliers


Markets

Market equilibrium occurs where the demand curve and the supply curve intersect. This is where there is no surplus or shortage. When equlibrium is not met, there is a temporary shortage, where demand is greater than supply, or surplus, where supply is greater than demand.
The rationing function of prices is the ability of competitive forces of demand and supply to establish a price of equilibrium.

Shifts in each curve will cause the equilibrium to shift as well.

A price ceiling is a price set (theoretically) below the equlibrium price that cannot be passed from below. A price floor is a price set (theoretically) above the equilibrium price that cannot be passed from below. There are limits set by the government that many economists argue inhibit the rationing function of prices and distort the allocation of resources. However, these are meant to assist in the protection of the rights of the people, either for the benefit of the company or the benefit of the consumers.
