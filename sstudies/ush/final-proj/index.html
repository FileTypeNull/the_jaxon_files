California Gold Rush
In 1848, gold was discovered in Sutter Creek near Coloma, California by James W. Marshall. This led to the California Gold Rush of 1848 to 1855; however, news about the mineral's discovery didn't reach the East until around 1849 which led to those who travelled to California in search of gold being nicknamed '49ers.

Lecompton Constitution
In 1857, the Lecompton Constitution was voted on for the first time. It was written by proslavery reporters after the Kansas-Nebraska Act of 1854 was passed, overturning the Missouri Compromise that declared that no states above the 32nd parallel could have slaves. Kansas and Nebraska were given the popular sovereignty to choose to be either a free or slave state. As a result of the Lecompton Constitution, the events of Bleeding Kansas, which was bloody violence between the free soilers and proslavery advocates in Kansas for the years leading up to the Civil War. The Lecompton Constitution was voted on twice more and was eventually rejected in 1858.

"Lecompton Konstitution." Kansas Memory, www.kansasmemory.org.

Missouri Compromise
In 1820, the Missouri Compromise was established, setting up the 32nd parallel which separated the free states from the slave states. It also required that there be an equal amount of slave states and free states, meaning that two states must be admitted together to keep this equilibrium. It resulted in extreme sectionist tension between the North and South throughout the antebellum period. Inevitably, the Missouri Compromise led to the Civil War because it determined a physical boundary between the North and South. Missouri was meant to be the only exception to the Missouri Compromise, but it was overturned in 1854 with the passage of the Kansas-Nebraska Act.

Dred Scott Cases
On April 6th, 1846, Dred Scott filed a lawsuit against Irene Emerson, his owner, for unlawful ownership. Because his old master had died while he was in free territory, he believed that by Missouri statutes he was no longer to be considered a slave. However, Irene, his master's wife, picked up Scott and took him back to St. Louis despite his claims at his new status as a freedman Unfortunately, in 1857, the case made its way to the Supreme Court, where they declared that African Americans were not citizens and were protected under the right of property to their master's families. This heightened sectionist tension between the North and South due to its controversial decision that devastated the attempts of abolitionists to attack slavery as immoral due to the now illegitimate citizenship of any freedmen.

"Dred Scott Cases, 1846-1857." Missouri Digital Heritage, www.sos.mo.gov.

Fugitive Slave Act of 1850
After the war with Mexico in 1848, the United States gained a massive amount of territory from Mexico known as the Mexican cession. This new land included parts of modern New Mexico, Arizona, Utah, Nevada, and Colorado. Consequently, the question over the potential status of slavery within these territories led to tension within Congress that was supposedly resolved by Henry Clay's Compromise of 1850. However, this compromise only added further to the sectionist conflict that led to the Civil War due to its provisions. One such provision was the Fugitive Slave Act, which forced the North to participate in the returning of slaves to the South. Between 1850 and 1860, nearly 343 African Americans appeared before federal commissioners, and 332 of those were returned to the South.

"Fugitive Slave Law of 1850." Ohio History Central, www.ohiohistorycentral.org.

John Brown's Raid on Harpers Ferry
On October 17th, 1859, John Brown, an abolitionist, led a group of his supporters to Harpers Ferry, Virginia. They captured affluent citizens and seized the federal armory and arsenal. His goal was to have the slaves of the town join him in his raid, but this did not occur, and most of his army was either killed or captured. He was senteneced to death for treason, murder, and insurrection and hanged on December 2nd, 1859.

"John Brown's Harpers Ferry Raid." American Battlefield Trust, www.battlefields.org.

Presidential Election of 1860
The 1860 election was significant in that the first Republican president had been elected: Abraham Lincoln. This gravely upset the South, since Lincoln's name did not even appear on their ballot due to the legislature's distaste for such a candidate. Lincoln was not an abolitionist, but a free soiler; thus, he wanted to prevent the expansion of slavery into further territory. As a result, the South feared that the sequence of evewnts that followed would lead to the end of slavery—in other words, their "right" to property would be revoked. Inevitably, this led to the secession of the South from the United States and the formation of the Confederacy, inciting what would eventually become the American Civil War.

Nullification Crisis of 1832-1833
Andrew Jackson, in 1832, signed into law a tariff that lowered the burden of the 1828 tariff but not significantly enough to appease the South. The South wanted smaller tariffs because they made a profit off of trade with Europe. In response, South Carolina retaliated by declaring the tariffs of 1828 and 1832 unconstitutional and threatening secession. The sitting Vice President, John C. Calhoun, quit his office in order to defend the position of South Carolina. This caused great tension between the North and South regarding the issue, and threatened the dissolution of the Union. Luckily, Henry Clay resolved the issue temporarily with the Compromise Tariff of 1833, which lowered the tariffs significantly and voided the previous two.

Seneca Falls Convention
The Seneca Falls Convention in 1848 was the first women's rights convention in America's history. At the convention were several prominent abolitionists, women's rights activists, and others. Unfortunately, Frederick Douglass was the sole African American attendee. At the convention, the Declaration of Sentiments—a callback to the Declaration of Independence—was created, stating the tribulations of women and slaves in the United States and how prejudice against any one group is unlawful and unjust. Although there was little success in the convention other than increasing awareness of issues, the Seneca Falls Convention represents a landmark in American history for its celebration of women's activism and participation in society during the Antebellum era.
